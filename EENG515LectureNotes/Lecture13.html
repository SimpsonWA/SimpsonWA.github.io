Range Space

Let \( A:X\rightarrow Y \) be a linear operator between 2 vector spaces \( X \) and \( Y \), the Range or range space of \( A \) (denoted \( \cal{R}(A) \)) is:

\[ \cal{R}(A):=\{\vec{y} \in Y: A\vec{x}=\vec{y} \quad \text{for some} \ \vec{x}\in X\} \]

The range space is a linear subspace of \( Y \).

Null Space

Let \( A:X\rightarrow Y \) be a linear operator between 2 vector spaces \( X \) and \( Y \). The Null Space or Null denoted \( \cal{N}(A) \) is:

\[ \cal{N}(A):=\{\vec{x} \in X: A\vec{x}=\vec{0} \in Y\} \]

The null space is a linear subspace of \( X \).

Four Fundamental Subspaces of a Linear Operator

When \( X \) and \( Y \) are inner product spaces, we can talk about the Range Space of \( A^{*} \) (which is the linear subspace of \( X \)) and the Null Space of \( A^{*} \) (which is a linear subspace of \( Y \)). We then have 4 Fundamental Subspaces of a Linear Operator:

\[ \begin{align*} \cal{R}(A) & := \{\vec{y} \in Y: A\vec{x}=\vec{y} \quad \text{for some} \ \vec{x}\in X\} \\ \cal{N}(A) & :=\{\vec{x} \in X: A\vec{x}=\vec{0} \in Y\} \\ \cal{R}(A^{*}) & :=\{\vec{x} \in X: A^{*}\vec{y}=\vec{x} \quad \text{for some} \ \vec{y}\in Y\} \\ \cal{N}(A^{*}) & :=\{\vec{y} \in Y: A^{*}\vec{x}=\vec{0} \in Y\} \end{align*} \]

Fundamental Subspaces: Matrix Case

Again, consider a case where \( X = \mathbb{R}^n \) and \( Y = \mathbb{R}^m \). Recall \( A:X\rightarrow Y \) can be represented as multiplication by an \( m \times n \) matrix.

When \( A \) is a matrix, \( \cal{R}(A) \) is just the span of columns of \( A \) and \( \cal{R}(A^{*}) \) is just the span of rows of \( A \)

\[ \begin{align} \cal{R}(A) = \text{colspan}(A) \\ \cal{R}(A^*) = \text{rowspan}(A) \end{align} \]

Matrix Rank: The Rank of a matrix equals the dimension of its column span and the dimension of its row span:

\[ \text{rank}(A) = \text{dim}(\cal{R}(A)) = \text{dim}(\cal{R}(A^{*})) \]

We say that \( A \) is

  • Full Rank: if \( \text{rank}(A) = \min\{m,n\} \)
  • Rank Deficient if it is not full rank

The rank of \( A \) relates to the dimensions of its four fundamental subspaces:

  • \( \text{dim}(\cal{R}(A)) = \text{rank}(A) = \text{dim(colspan(A))} \)
  • \( \text{dim}(\cal{N}(A)) = n - \text{rank}(A) \)
  • \( \text{dim}(\cal{R}(A^*)) = \text{rank}(A) = \text{dim(rowspan(A))} \)
  • \( \text{dim}(\cal{N}(A^*)) = m - \text{rank}(A) \)

Note for any \( m \times n \) matrix \( A \):

  • \( \text{dim}(\cal{R}(A)) + \text{dim}(\cal{N}(A)) = n \) (number of columns of \( A \))
  • \( \text{dim}(\cal{R}(A^*)) + \text{dim}(\cal{N}(A^*)) = m \) (number of rows of \( A \))
  • \( \text{dim}(\cal{R}(A)) + \text{dim}(\cal{N}(A^*)) = m \) (number of rows of \( A \))
  • \( \text{dim}(\cal{R}(A^*)) + \text{dim}(\cal{N}(A)) = n \) (number of columns of \( A \))

Additionally for 2 matrices \( A \) and \( B \):

  • \( \text{rank}(AB) \leq \min\{\text{rank}(A), \text{rank}(B)\} \)
  • \( \text{rank}(A+B) \leq \text{rank}(A) + \text{rank}(B) \)

Projection Operators

A linear operator \( P:X\rightarrow X \) from a vector space \( X \) into itself is called a projection operator or idempotent if:

\[ P^2 = P \]

Orthogonal Projection: A projection operator \( P \) in an inner product space \( X \) is called an orthogonal projection if \( \cal{R}(P) \perp \cal{N}(P) \) (i.e., if \( \langle \vec{x}, \vec{y} \rangle = 0 \) for all \( \vec{x} \in \cal{R}(P) \) or \( \vec{x} \in \cal{N}(P) \))

A bounded linear operator \( P: X\rightarrow X \) on an inner product space \( X \) is an orthogonal projection if:

  • P is Idempotent: \( P^2 = P \)
  • P is Self-adjoint: \( P = P^* \)

As mentioned before, the minimizing vector \( \vec{x}' \) is referred to as the orthogonal projection of \( \vec{x} \) onto \( T \).

  • In other words, \( \vec{x}' = P \vec{x} \) where P is an orthogonal projection operator with \( \cal{R}(P) = T \)

Projection Operators: Matrix Case

Consider \( X = \mathbb{R}^n \) or \( \mathbb{C}^n \) and \( P:X\rightarrow X \) can be represented as multiplication by an \( n \times n \) matrix. Then:

  • An \( n \times n \) matrix \( P \) is a projection if \( P^2 = P \)
  • An \( n \times n \) matrix \( P \) is an orthogonal projection if \( P^2 = P \) and \( P^{H} = P \)

There is a recipe for constructing projection matrices in \( X = \mathbb{R}^n \) or \( \mathbb{C}^n \). Consider a set of \( m \) linearly independent vectors \( \vec{v}_1, \vec{v}_2, \ldots, \vec{v}_n \) in \( \mathbb{R}^n \) or \( \mathbb{C}^n \).

We can construct an orthogonal projection matrix \( P \) onto the subspace \( T \) which spans the vectors as follows:

  • Construct an \( n \times n \) matrix where \( \text{colspan}(A) = T \)

\[ A = [\vec{v_1} \ \vec{v_2} \ \ldots \ \vec{v_n}] \]

Let:

  • \[ P = A(A^{H}A)^{-1}A^{H} \]
