Best Approximation to a Vector

  • For an orthonormal collection of vectors \( \vec{v_1}, \vec{v_2}, \ldots, \vec{v_n} \) in an inner product space \( S \) and an arbitrary vector \( \vec{x} \in S \), the best approximation \( \vec{x'} \) to \( \vec{x} \) among all vectors in \( T = \text{span}\{\vec{v_1}, \vec{v_2}, \ldots, \vec{v_n}\} \) is given by:
    \[ \vec{x'} = \sum_{k=1}^{n} <\vec{x}, \vec{v_k}> \vec{v_k} \]
  • For an expansion, we want to consider a case where \( \vec{v_1}, \vec{v_2}, \ldots, \vec{v_n} \) form an orthonormal basis for the entire space \( S \):
    • In this case, \( \vec{x'} = \vec{x} \) so:
      \[ \vec{x} = \sum_{k=1}^{n} <\vec{x}, \vec{v_k}> \vec{v_k} \]
    • Notice that \( \vec{x} \) is on both sides of this equation, so it is known as a Reproducing Vector
  • If \( S \) is infinite-dimensional and we have an infinite sequence of orthonormal vectors \( \vec{v_1}, \vec{v_2}, \ldots, \vec{v_n} \) that form a basis of \( S \) as long as
    \[ \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} |<\vec{x}, \vec{v_k}>|^2 < \infty \]
    we can also write
    \[ \vec{x} = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} <\vec{x}, \vec{v_k}> \vec{v_k} \]
    • Where the transform coefficients are \( <\vec{x}, \vec{v_1}>, <\vec{x}, \vec{v_2}>, \ldots \)

Perseval's Theorem

Setup

  • \( S \) is a finite-dimensional inner product space; we denote the inner product of \( S \) by \( <*,*>_s \)
  • Suppose \( \vec{v_1}, \vec{v_2}, \ldots, \vec{v_n} \) form an orthonormal basis for \( S \)
  • Suppose that \( \vec{x} \) and \( \vec{y} \) are two arbitrary vectors in \( S \) and denote their transform coefficients as
    \[ \alpha_k = <\vec{x}, \vec{v_k}>_s \quad \text{and} \quad \beta_k = <\vec{y}, \vec{v_k}>_s \quad \text{for } k = 1,2, \ldots, n \]
  • Let \( \vec{\alpha} \) and \( \vec{\beta} \) denote vectors by:
    \[ \vec{\alpha} = \begin{bmatrix} \alpha_1 \\ \alpha_2 \\ \vdots \\ \alpha_n \end{bmatrix} \quad \text{and} \quad \vec{\beta} = \begin{bmatrix} \beta_1 \\ \beta_2 \\ \vdots \\ \beta_n \end{bmatrix} \]

Perseval's Theorem

\[ <\vec{x}, \vec{y}>_s = \sum_{k=1}^{n} \alpha_k \beta_k^{*} = <\vec{\alpha}, \vec{\beta}>_{l2} \]

Properties

  • By taking \( \vec{x} = \vec{y} \) thus \( \vec{\beta} = \vec{\alpha} \) we obtain:
    \[ ||\vec{x}||_s^2 = ||\vec{\alpha}||_{l2}^2 \]
  • The vectors \( \vec{x} \) and \( \vec{y} \) are orthogonal in \( S \) if \( \vec{\alpha} \) and \( \vec{\beta} \) are orthogonal in \( l_2 \)

Truncating and Quantizing Orthobasis Expansions

  • Truncating: One common type of manipulation involves transforming a signal to some domain, setting some coefficients to zero, and inverting the transform.
  • Quantizing: Another common type of manipulation involves transforming a signal to some domain and then quantizing the transform coefficients so they can be encoded in binary.
